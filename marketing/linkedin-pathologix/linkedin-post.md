# PathoLogix LinkedIn Post

## Recommended Post

EMT education should train decision-making, not just answer recall.

I have been building PathoLogix, an interactive training platform designed to help EMT students practice the judgment behind patient care.

The platform now brings several learning modes together:

- A 3D EMT Scene Lab where students assess hazards, request resources, and work through patient care in the correct sequence
- Clinical scenarios that highlight important cues and explain why one decision is stronger than the alternatives
- Timed NREMT-style exam practice
- Focused flashcards and progress tracking for weaker topics

One of the most interesting challenges has been making the simulator feel like a real process. The user should need to observe the scene, recognize what matters, choose an assessment area, and then select the right action for that specific patient. Every interaction should reinforce the workflow they will need in the field.

PathoLogix is still evolving, but it is becoming the kind of EMT study experience I wanted to see: practical, visual, and built around clinical reasoning.

I would especially value feedback from EMT students, working EMS professionals, and educators. What would make an interactive training tool genuinely useful in your learning or teaching?

## Shorter Alternative

I have been building PathoLogix, an interactive EMT training platform focused on the judgment behind patient care.

Students can work through a 3D emergency scene, identify hazards, complete assessments in sequence, practice NREMT-style questions, review clinical rationales, and reinforce weaker topics with flashcards.

The goal is simple: move EMT preparation beyond memorizing answers and toward practicing how to observe, prioritize, and act.

I would love feedback from EMT students, EMS professionals, and educators. What would you want to see in a tool like this?
